Welcome 👋

This is a self-paced, interactive course on Geometric Dimensioning and Tolerancing (GD&T) per ASME Y14.5 — the language used on engineering drawings to say not just how big a feature is, but what shape it must have, how it must be oriented, and where it must be.

It's written for someone exactly like you: a mechanical engineering background, comfortable with tolerances like 25.4 ±0.1, but new to the symbols, frames, and datum letters that cover modern drawings. No CAD required — everything here runs in your browser.

How to use this course
  • Work through the modules in order — each builds on the last.
  • Play with every interactive widget. They're the fastest way to build intuition.
  • Mark each module complete when you're done; your progress is saved in this browser.
  • Finish with the quiz, and keep the cheat sheet handy afterwards.

1 · Why GD&T Exists

You already know coordinate (±) tolerancing. GD&T exists because ± tolerancing alone can't fully describe what makes a part function — and it sometimes rejects perfectly good parts.

The problem with ± everywhere

Say a hole is located 20 ±0.1 from the left edge and 20 ±0.1 from the bottom edge. Three problems hide in that simple callout:

  1. The tolerance zone is a square. The hole center may be 0.1 mm off in X and 0.1 mm off in Y (corner of the square, 0.141 mm from nominal) and still pass — but a hole 0.12 mm off along one axis fails. Distance from nominal is what matters for a mating pin, and the square treats equal distances unequally.
  2. Which edges, exactly? Real edges aren't perfect. If the left edge is bowed, where do you measure from? ± dims don't say. GD&T answers this with datums.
  3. It says nothing about form or orientation. A face can be "25 ±0.1 thick" and still be wavy, tilted, or twisted in ways that ruin assembly.

Try it: square zone vs. cylindrical zone

Below is the classic comparison. The square is the ±0.1/±0.1 coordinate zone; the circle is a position tolerance of Ø0.283 — sized to circumscribe the square. Click (or drag) anywhere to place a measured hole center and see how each system judges it.

The circle accepts every center the square accepts, plus the corners-of-equal-distance the square irrationally rejects — 57% more usable tolerance area, for free, with no loss of function. That's the flagship argument for position tolerancing.

What GD&T adds

  • Function-shaped tolerance zones — cylinders for hole axes, parallel planes for faces, 3D boundaries for complex profiles.
  • Datums — an explicit, repeatable answer to "measure from where?", mirroring how the part actually sits in the assembly or fixture.
  • Separate control of size, form, orientation, and location — each toleranced only as tightly as function demands.
  • Bonus tolerance — extra location tolerance when a hole or pin is made favorably, matching the physics of clearance fits (module 8).
Key takeaways
  • GD&T supplements ± size tolerances; it doesn't replace them. Size still gets ± (or limit) dimensions.
  • Round (cylindrical) position zones match function and give 57% more area than the equivalent square.
  • The governing standard in the US is ASME Y14.5 (2009 and 2018 editions are both common); ISO has an equivalent system (ISO 1101 / GPS) with mostly-identical symbols.

3 · Feature Control Frames

The feature control frame (FCF) is the sentence of the GD&T language. Every geometric tolerance on a drawing lives in one of these rectangular frames, attached by a leader to a surface or to a size dimension.

Anatomy of a frame

Hover over (or tap) each compartment:

Read left to right, an FCF always says the same kind of sentence:

"This feature's [characteristic] must fall within a [shape] tolerance zone of size [value] [at this material condition], established relative to datums [A, then B, then C]."

Where the frame points matters

  • Leader to a surface → the tolerance controls the surface itself.
  • Attached to a size dimension (like Ø10.0 ±0.2) → the tolerance controls the axis or center plane of that feature of size. This is how position controls a hole's axis rather than its wall.

Two default rules you must know

  • Rule #1 — the Envelope Principle: for a feature of size, the size limits also control form. A shaft at its largest allowed size (MMC) must have perfect form — it must fit inside a perfect envelope at MMC. As it's made smaller, it may bend/bow by the amount it departed from MMC. So size tolerance alone already gives you some free form control.
  • Rule #2 — RFS by default: if no or modifier appears, the tolerance applies Regardless of Feature Size (RFS): the stated value is what you get, no matter the actual size of the feature.

Build your own frame

Assemble an FCF below and watch the plain-English translation update. Notice how the builder stops you from doing illegal things (form tolerances can't reference datums; runout can't take MMC).

Key takeaways
  • FCF order is always: symbol → zone size (with Ø if cylindrical, and modifier if any) → datum references in order of precedence.
  • Basic dimensions (numbers in boxes, e.g. 20) are theoretically exact and carry no ± tolerance — the FCF supplies the tolerance for them.
  • What the frame attaches to decides whether it controls a surface or an axis/center plane.

4 · Datums

Datums answer the question ± dimensioning dodges: measure from where? They define the coordinate system the part is inspected in — and, done right, it's the same coordinate system the part experiences in the assembly.

Datum vs. datum feature

  • A datum feature is a real, imperfect surface on the part, labeled with a boxed letter on the drawing (Ⓐ on a stem: A).
  • A datum is the theoretical perfect plane, axis, or point derived from that surface — what a perfect granite table or gauge pin "extracts" when it contacts the imperfect surface.
  • A datum reference frame (DRF) is the full coordinate system built from (usually) three datums — three mutually perpendicular planes.

The 3-2-1 rule

A rigid body has six degrees of freedom: translation in X, Y, Z and rotation about each. A three-plane DRF removes them in sequence:

  • Primary datum — the part rests on it with a minimum of 3 points of contact → kills 1 translation + 2 rotations.
  • Secondary datum — slid against with 2 points → kills 1 translation + 1 rotation.
  • Tertiary datum — 1 point → kills the last translation.

Try it: datum precedence

The order of letters in the FCF is not decoration — it's the clamping sequence. Change the order below and watch how the same imperfect part sits differently in the gauge.

Choosing datums well

  • Pick datum features that mate first in assembly — the mounting face, the pilot bore, the locating pin holes.
  • Primary should be the surface with the biggest, most stable contact.
  • Datum features can be planes or features of size: a bore used as a datum yields a datum axis; a slot yields a center plane.
Key takeaways
  • Datum order = fixturing order. and are different requirements that can pass/fail different parts.
  • 3-2-1: primary seats, secondary aligns, tertiary stops.
  • Datums are theoretical; datum features are real and imperfect.

5 · Form Tolerances

Form tolerances control the shape of a feature by itself — no datums, ever. They refine the free form control you already get from Rule #1 when size limits alone aren't tight enough.

Straightness — each line element of a surface must lie between two parallel lines a tolerance apart. Applied to an axis (with Ø), the whole axis must fit in a tolerance cylinder.
Flatness — the entire surface must lie between two parallel planes a tolerance apart. The planes float at any angle: flatness ignores orientation.
Circularity (roundness) — at each cross-section independently, the surface must lie between two concentric circles whose radii differ by the tolerance.
Cylindricity — the entire surface must lie between two coaxial cylinders whose radii differ by the tolerance. It's circularity + straightness + taper control in one.

Watch the fine print: circularity and cylindricity tolerances are radial zone widths. A circularity tolerance of 0.05 means 0.05 between the two circles' radii — not diameters. Also note straightness applied to an axis with a Ø is the one form control that can be modified with and can override Rule #1's perfect-form-at-MMC envelope.
Key takeaways
  • Form = shape only. No datums. The zone floats to best fit the feature.
  • Flatness zone = two parallel planes; circularity = two concentric circles per slice; cylindricity = two coaxial cylinders.
  • A form tolerance must be tighter than the size tolerance to mean anything (Rule #1 already gives form control equal to the size tolerance).

6 · Orientation Tolerances

Orientation tolerances control tilt relative to a datum. The zone is still usually two parallel planes (or a cylinder for an axis) — but now the zone is locked at a specific angle to the datum instead of floating.

Perpendicularity — the zone is held at exactly 90° to the datum. Common on faces that must sit square, and on hole axes (with Ø) that must be square to a mounting face.
Parallelism — the zone is held at exactly 0° to the datum. Controls a face staying parallel to an opposite face, or an axis parallel to another axis.
Angularity — the zone is held at a basic angle (boxed, exact) to the datum. The angle itself carries no ±; the FCF value is the width of the zone.
An orientation tolerance also controls form. If a face must stay between two parallel planes 0.1 apart that are perpendicular to datum A, then that face is automatically also flat within 0.1. Orientation ⊃ form. (This nesting — location ⊃ orientation ⊃ form — is a core GD&T idea: each higher-level control buys you the ones below it, within its zone.)

Reading an example

attached to a Ø8.0–8.2 hole means: the hole's axis must fit inside a Ø0.1 cylindrical zone exactly perpendicular to datum A, with the zone growing by whatever the hole's size departs from MMC (Ø8.0). A gauge for this is a plate (datum A simulator) with a fixed perpendicular pin of Ø7.9 — the virtual condition. More on that in module 8.

Key takeaways
  • Orientation = tilt vs. a datum. Angularity uses a basic (boxed) angle; the tolerance is always a zone width, never degrees.
  • Perpendicularity and parallelism are just angularity at 90° and 0°.
  • Orientation zones also cap form error inside them.

7 · Position — the workhorse

Position is the most-used symbol in GD&T. It controls where the axis or center plane of a feature of size must be, relative to a datum reference frame.

How a position callout works

  1. Basic dimensions (boxed, exact) locate the true position — the theoretically perfect location of the hole's axis relative to the datums.
  2. The FCF, e.g. , defines a cylindrical tolerance zone (Ø0.25) centered on that true position, perpendicular to the primary datum, running the full depth of the hole.
  3. The hole's actual axis — the axis of the largest perfect pin that fits, roughly speaking — must lie entirely inside that cylinder.

Checking a measured part

Your CMM reports the hole center is off by ΔX and ΔY from true position. The diametral position deviation is:

deviation = 2 × √(ΔX² + ΔY²) — compare against the Ø tolerance

(The factor of 2 converts the radial offset into a diameter, to match the Ø zone.) Try it:

Why position beats ± location

  • Round zone matches function (module 1's 57% argument).
  • Datum sequence makes measurement repeatable.
  • With , it earns bonus tolerance and enables cheap fixed-pin functional gauges (next module).
  • Hole patterns can be toleranced as a group, controlling hole-to-hole spacing tightly while letting the whole pattern float more generously.
Key takeaways
  • Position needs basic dimensions to define true position, and (nearly always) datums.
  • Ø in the frame → cylindrical zone about the axis. No Ø → two parallel planes (used for slots/tabs, center planes).
  • Deviation = 2√(ΔX²+ΔY²). A hole 0.1 off in both X and Y is 0.283 off in position — worse than 0.12 off in X alone (0.24). Compare that to how the ± square zone judged the same two cases in module 1.

8 · MMC, LMC & Bonus Tolerance

Material condition modifiers are where GD&T stops being notation and starts being economics: they legally enlarge your tolerance zone when the part is made in a favorable direction.

Definitions

Hole (internal)Shaft (external)Mnemonic
MMC
Maximum Material Condition		Smallest holeLargest shaftThe heaviest the part can be
LMC
Least Material Condition		Largest holeSmallest shaftThe lightest the part can be

The physics of bonus tolerance

A clearance pin must pass through a hole. The worst case is the smallest hole (MMC). If the hole happens to be drilled bigger than MMC, there is physically more room — the hole could be further off-location and the pin would still fit. The modifier writes that physical truth into the tolerance:

total position tolerance = stated tolerance + (actual size − MMC size) [for a hole]

The second term is the bonus. At MMC the bonus is zero; at LMC it's the entire size tolerance.

Virtual condition — the gauge-maker's number

The virtual condition (VC) is the worst-case boundary generated by size and geometric tolerance combined:

  • Hole with tolerance at MMC: VC = MMC − geo tol → for the example above, 10.0 − 0.2 = Ø9.8. A fixed gauge pin of Ø9.8 at true position checks the whole requirement in one plunge — no CMM needed.
  • Shaft with tolerance at MMC: VC = MMC + geo tol → the smallest hole that always clears it.

When to use which

  • — clearance-fit assembly: bolts through holes, pins into slots. The most common modifier by far. Cheapest to inspect (functional gauges) and to make (bonus).
  • — protecting a minimum wall/edge distance: a hole near an edge, a boss that must retain material for a later machining op.
  • RFS (no modifier — the default per Rule #2) — when function depends on the actual center regardless of size: press fits, balance-critical rotating parts, centering.
Key takeaways
  • Bonus = |actual size − MMC size|, added to the stated tolerance, only when is present.
  • Virtual condition (hole, at MMC) = MMC − tol; it's the size of the functional gauge pin.
  • Modifiers apply only to features of size (things with a dimension and an axis/center plane) — never to a plain surface.

9 · Profile Tolerances

Profile is GD&T's most powerful — and, on modern drawings, increasingly dominant — control. It wraps a uniform 3D boundary around any shape, including curves and freeform surfaces that nothing else can tolerance.

Profile of a line — controls each 2D cross-section independently (like circularity generalized to any curve).
Profile of a surface — controls the entire surface as one 3D requirement (like cylindricity generalized to any shape). This is the one you'll see most.

How the zone works

  • The true profile is defined entirely by basic dimensions (or the CAD model itself).
  • The tolerance value is the total width of a zone centered on the true profile — by default split equally, half outside, half inside (equal bilateral).
  • Unequal split is shown with the modifier (2018) — e.g. 1.0 Ⓤ 0.8 means 1.0 total, 0.8 of it outward.
  • With datums → profile controls location + orientation + form all at once. Without datums → form only.

Why it's taking over

In model-based drawings, a single note like "unless otherwise specified" can tolerance every surface of the part relative to the CAD model in one shot, with tighter callouts only where function demands. One frame replaces dozens of ± dimensions.

Key takeaways
  • Profile = a uniform-thickness boundary around the exact (basic/CAD) shape.
  • The value is a total zone width, default equally disposed about the true profile.
  • Datums optional: with them it locates; without them it's pure form for any shape.

10 · Runout

Runout is the tolerance for rotating parts: shafts, spindles, pulleys, anything that spins about a datum axis. It's defined by its inspection method — chuck the part on its datum axis, spin it, and read a dial indicator.

Circular runout — at each axial position independently, the total indicator swing (FIM — full indicator movement) per revolution must not exceed the tolerance. Catches eccentricity and out-of-round at each slice.
Total runout — the indicator is also swept along the surface; every reading over the whole surface must stay within one band. Adds control of taper and straightness. Zone = two coaxial cylinders about the datum axis.

Try it: spin the part

This shaft's outer surface is eccentric to the datum axis (exaggerated). Watch the indicator; the FIM is the runout reading. Note FIM = 2 × eccentricity.

Runout rules: always requires a datum axis; always applies RFS (no allowed — you physically spin the actual part); circular runout simultaneously limits circularity and coaxiality, total runout adds cylindricity. Runout is a cheap, functional composite check for anything rotating.
Key takeaways
  • Circular = per-slice FIM; Total = whole-surface band.
  • Datum axis required; RFS only.
  • FIM from pure eccentricity e is 2e — the indicator sees the high side and the low side each revolution.

11 · Concentricity & Symmetry (legacy)

Two symbols you'll meet on older drawings — and should almost never put on new ones. ASME Y14.5-2018 deleted both.

Concentricity — required all diametrically-opposed median points of a surface of revolution to lie within a cylindrical zone about the datum axis. Note: median points, not the surface — which made it brutally expensive to inspect (you can't check it with a gauge or a simple indicator sweep).
Symmetry — the same median-point idea for slots/tabs about a datum center plane. Same inspection pain.

What to use instead

If the function is…Use
Parts must assemble coaxially (fit)Position at MMC
Rotating part must not wobble (dynamic balance-ish, sealing, bearing seats)Runout /
True mass-distribution balanceAn actual balance spec — not GD&T
Slot/tab centered for fitPosition (no Ø → parallel-plane zone) at MMC
Key takeaways
  • If you see or on a drawing, it's pre-2018 (or ISO, where concentricity lives on). Ask what function was intended; it's usually position or runout in disguise.
  • Don't specify them on new ASME drawings.

The rules

  • Rule #1: size limits control form — perfect form required at MMC (the envelope).
  • Rule #2: RFS applies unless Ⓜ or Ⓛ is stated.
  • Basic dimensions (boxed) are exact; their tolerance comes from an FCF.
  • Datum order = fixturing order: 3 points primary, 2 secondary, 1 tertiary.
  • Control nesting: location ⊃ orientation ⊃ form — each also limits the ones below it.
